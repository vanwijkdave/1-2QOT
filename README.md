# 1-2QOT
Implementation of the 1-2 String QOT protocol and its variants, based on the SquidASM quantum entanglement simulator.

## Abstract:
> Current techniques for MPC rely on oblivious transfer (OT), whose standard constructions are based on current public key infrastructure (PKI), making them vulnerable to attacks by quantum computers. In this thesis, we investigate whether quantum oblivious transfer (QOT) can improve on classical OT in existing secure multiparty computation (MPC) protocols.

> We implement a 1-out-of-2 QOT protocol in the SquidASM quantum-network simulator and extend it to the different oblivious-transfer variants required by MPC. Our construction combines quantum communication with classical bit commitment, error correction, and hashing. Benchmarking under current and optimistic hardware assumptions shows that low-density parity-check (LDPC) codes outperform Reed–Solomon codes at lower leakage rates, and that Carter–Wegman hashing scales more efficiently in the number of bits than Toeplitz hashing. The classical post-processing, however, contributes only a small fraction of the total runtime, since the dominant cost is generating and measuring sufficiently many high-fidelity entangled pairs. For a realistic level of security, the protocol requires on the order of millions of entangled states, leading to execution times of weeks per oblivious transfer even under optimistic entanglement-generation rates. Although all OT variants needed for OT-based MPC can be constructed from our 1-2 QOT implementation, the current performance gap means that QOT is, for now, mainly a proof of concept rather than a practical alternative in deployed MPC systems.
